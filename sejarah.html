The History of Madura

The history of Madura began in the 13th century with the appointment of Arya Wiraraja as the first Adipati (Duke or Regent) of the island. In the ancient manuscript Nagarakretagama, particularly in Canto 15, it is mentioned that Madura was once considered united with the land of Java. This indicates that in the 14th century, around the year 1365, the Madurese and the Javanese were closely connected and were regarded as part of the same cultural community.

Between approximately 900 and 1500, Madura was under the influence of several major Hindu kingdoms in East Java, including Kediri, Singhasari, and Majapahit. These powerful kingdoms significantly influenced the governance, cultural development, and belief systems of the Madurese people. However, after the decline of Majapahit, the influence of Hindu kingdoms gradually weakened, and Madura began to experience the growing influence of Islamic kingdoms that developed along the northern coast of Java.

During the period between 1500 and 1624, the rulers of Madura largely depended on Islamic kingdoms located on the northern coast of Java, such as Demak, Gresik, and Surabaya. This shows the strong influence of Islamic political powers in shaping the government and social life of Madura. In 1624, Madura was eventually conquered by the Mataram Sultanate, which was at the peak of its power at that time, marking an important turning point in the island’s political history.

In the first half of the 18th century, Madura came under Dutch colonial rule. The island became part of the territories controlled by the Dutch East India Company and was later governed directly by the Dutch East Indies administration. During this period, Dutch influence became stronger in governance and economic activities. Nevertheless, the Madurese people continued to maintain their cultural identity and traditions. In the administrative division of the 1920s, Madura officially became part of East Java in Indonesia.

Mamapar Gigi

the word “mapar” means to straighten or arrange neatly. Therefore, mapar gigi refers to the process of aligning the teeth of a bride using a special tool. However, its meaning goes beyond aesthetics; it also symbolizes the removal of negative elements or bad influences before entering married life.

Ojhung

Ojhung is a traditional performance of the people of Madura. especially in Sumenep. This tradition is usually performed during long dry seasons. The main purpose of the Ojhung ritual is to pray for and invite rain.

Karapan Sapi

Karapan Sapi is a traditional bull racing competition originating from Madura, East Java. The event is generally held in August and September, with the final round usually taking place in Pamekasan to compete for the President’s Rolling Trophy.

Carok

Carok is a traditional practice in Madura that is associated with restoring personal honor when it is violated by others, often related to wealth, power, or women. To reclaim their dignity after being insulted or humiliated, individuals may engage in carok, a practice that has historically received support from the surrounding social environment. In this confrontation, the participants commonly use a celurit, a traditional curved blade that is considered a symbol of masculinity and bravery among Madurese men.

Toktok

Toktok is a strength competition between two male bulls that face each other and clash their horns. In this tradition, the strength and endurance of each bull are tested. Because it involves significant risk, the competition must be guided and supervised by experienced handlers or experts.

Rokat Tase

Rokat Tase’ is a traditional ritual practiced by coastal communities in Madura, especially among fishing communities. It is performed as an expression of gratitude for the abundance of marine resources and as a prayer for safety before going out to sea. This tradition is understood as a form of thanksgiving to the Creator for the blessings and protection that have been given.

Moang Sangkal

Moang Sangkal is one of the iconic traditional dances of Sumenep in Madura. This Madurese traditional dance carries spiritual and symbolic meanings as a ritual to ward off misfortune and as a prayer for safety and blessings. It is also commonly performed as a welcoming dance to greet guests at various cultural events.

Gambu

Gambu is a dance that developed from the gambu performing arts tradition, a folk performance that combines dance, music, and elements of storytelling or entertainment. This dance is part of a performance that reflects and portrays the daily life and culture of the people of Madura.

Saronen

Saronen is a traditional musical instrument from Madura that is closely associated with the Karapan Sapi. The saronen is a cone-shaped wind instrument made from teak wood, featuring six holes on the front and one on the back. The end part is made from siwalan palm leaves and is decorated with a coconut shell ornament shaped like a mustache, symbolizing strength and bravery.

Tong-tong

Tong-Tong is a traditional percussion instrument from Madura made from wood. This art form was originally used to wake people for sahur during the month of Ramadan and first developed in the area of Pasongsongan, Sumenep.

Gili Labak

Gili Labak is a small island that has become one of the popular tourist destinations in Madura. It is located in the Sumenep Regency, in the southeastern part of the island. The island is well known for its crystal-clear beaches, white sand, and beautiful ocean scenery.

Arosbaya Limestone Hill

The area, also known as Pelalangan Hill, is located in Berbeluk Village, Arosbaya District, Bangkalan, on Madura. Unlike most limestone hills that are usually white, Bukit Kapur Arosbaya has a distinctive reddish-brown color, which makes it unique and visually striking.

Pemandian Sumber Pocong

Located in Pocong Village, Bangkalan, on Madura, Pemandian Sumber Pocong is one of the best tourist destinations in Madura that is worth visiting. Despite its name sounding rather frightening, Sumber Pocong offers a peaceful and refreshing natural retreat for visitors. The area around the bathing site remains natural and is surrounded by lush trees.

Telaga Kermata

The lake located in Saronggi, Sumenep, offers a very soothing and refreshing view. Telaga Kertamata was originally a natural spring. During the Dutch colonial period, it was later transformed into an artificial lake and used as an irrigation system for the surrounding rice fields.

Kapal Jodoh

Kapal Jodoh is a ship-shaped tourist icon in Sumenep, Madura. It has become a popular photo spot and a symbol of hope for finding a soulmate, while also serving as a place to enjoy the beauty of the beach.

Toroan Waterfall

Located in Ketapang Village, Sampang, Toroan Waterfall is a unique waterfall. Unlike most waterfalls that flow into rivers, the water from Toroan Waterfall flows directly from the cliff into the waters of the Java Sea.

Geger Hill

Bukit Geger is one of the natural tourist attractions located in Bangkalan, in the western part of Madura. This place is well known for its beautiful hill landscapes and its relatively natural and peaceful atmosphere.

Gili Iyang

Gili Iyang is a small island located in Sumenep, in the eastern part of Madura. The island is famous for having very high oxygen levels, which is why it is often referred to as one of the places with the cleanest air in Indonesia.

Jaddih Hill

Bukit Jaddih is one of the well-known natural tourist destinations in Bangkalan, located on Madura. This place was originally a limestone mining area, but it later became a tourist attraction due to its unique and fascinating landscape.
